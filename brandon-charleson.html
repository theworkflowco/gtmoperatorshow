Discussion Framework

We've identified several areas where your experience could provide unique value to GTM operators:

Topic 1: From Military Operations to GTM Integration Architecture

Why this is interesting: You bring 13 years of Air Force operational discipline to GTM automation. Most operators don't have military systems thinking—understanding how you translate mission-critical frameworks into integration ecosystems could reveal patterns GTM teams are missing.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You've written about how military values—"service first, show up, stay ready"—aren't just military values, they're life values. How do these operational frameworks actually show up when you're designing integration layers for a GTM system? What patterns from Air Force operations apply when the "mission" is revenue?
  • Most GTM teams don't think in terms of "mission-critical" systems. When you look at how companies build their GTM stack, what would never survive in military operations? What's the equivalent of mission failure in GTM?

Topic 2: Integration Ecosystems vs Point Solutions (Your 2026 Prediction)

Why this is interesting: You've predicted 2026 will be about "the rise of the integration ecosystem"—systematic connection layers that make point solutions actually scale. Most teams are still connecting tools without thinking architecturally. This is a shift from tactical tool adoption to strategic infrastructure.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You wrote that "integrations and partnerships are never more important than now to stay relevant" and that every SaaS will integrate with Chatbots via MCP or Connectors. Walk me through what changed in your thinking—when did you realize this wasn't about better tools, but about the connection layer between them?
  • When does a company need to stop thinking about individual tools and start thinking architecturally about their integration ecosystem? What's the inflection point where tactical integration becomes strategic infrastructure?

Topic 3: Token Efficiency and Constraint-Driven AI Design

Why this is interesting: Your TOON format experiments show that constraining tokens actually improves output quality. You've implemented this in Instantly.ai's MCP Server and measured real savings. This counter-intuitive finding—that less can produce more—reveals something important about how GTM operators should think about AI system design.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You're testing TOON format because "it aims to simplify the data structure by being more token efficient" and you're already seeing "improvements in how responses are handled, thus saving tokens." Walk me through what you learned when you first constrained the structure—what surprised you about how the AI responded to less?
  • Most teams approach AI prompts by adding more context and examples. Your work suggests the opposite—that constraint forces clarity. What does this reveal about how GTM operators should be designing their AI-native workflows?

Topic 4: Builder Mindset—Creating What Doesn't Exist

Why this is interesting: You've built multiple n8n community nodes because "integration gaps are where GTM operations break down." When the connectors don't exist, you build them. This "build vs buy" decision framework is critical for GTM operators deciding when to extend their tools versus accept constraints.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You wrote that you've "built several n8n community nodes that I can basically do them in my sleep." Walk me through your decision framework—how do you know when to build versus when to accept the limitation? What signals tell you "this gap is worth solving"?
  • Most GTM leaders aren't technical builders. How should a non-technical operator think about when to extend their tools versus when to work within constraints? Where's the line between configuration and engineering?
