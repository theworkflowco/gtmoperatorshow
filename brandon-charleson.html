About the GTM Operator Show

GTM Operator Show features conversations with operators who are pushing the boundaries of how GTM teams work—exploring the thinking patterns and decision frameworks behind meaningful innovation.

The Audience

The show reaches ~10,000 highly curated GTM leaders on LinkedIn—practitioners and leaders at tech startups who are actively seeking better approaches to revenue operations, process design, and systematic execution. These are operators who care about how things work, not just what to do.

Format

  • Platform: Squadcast (browser-based, no download or account needed)
  • Duration: 50-60 minutes of conversation (edited down for the final episode)
  • Style: Video podcast, conversational—just you and the host talking through the topics
  • Timing flexibility: If you have a product launch or time-sensitive announcement, we can coordinate your episode timing to align with your promotional window

Discussion Framework

We've identified several areas where your experience could provide unique value to GTM operators:

Topic 1: Experimentation Mindset—What Drives You to Test New Approaches

Why this is interesting: You're constantly experimenting—TOON format for token efficiency, open-source AI models when closed-source won't work, new integration patterns. Most operators stick with proven approaches. Your willingness to test reveals a different relationship with risk and learning.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You're constantly experimenting—TOON format, AI models, n8n nodes. What drives your decision to experiment with something versus stick with what works? When do you know something is worth the time to test?
  • Have any of these experiments surprised you—gone differently than you expected? What did that teach you about how you approach new tools or methods?
  • Most GTM operators wait for best practices to emerge before adopting new approaches. What's an unpopular opinion you have about how teams should think about experimentation?
Topic 2: Integration Ecosystems—Predicting 2026 Before Others See It

Why this is interesting: You predicted 2026 will be about integration ecosystems, not individual tools. Most teams are still thinking tactically about tool selection. This prediction reveals you're seeing architectural patterns emerge that others are missing.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You predicted 2026 will be about integration ecosystems—systematic connection layers, not just better point solutions. What made you see this before others? What are you noticing that most operators aren't?
  • What are you building NOW because of this prediction that most operators aren't? How does seeing this shift change what you prioritize in your work?
  • When you look at a company's tech stack, what tells you they're thinking architecturally versus just connecting tools? What's the difference in how their systems perform?
Topic 3: Building for Legacy SaaS—MCP as the Modernization Layer

Why this is interesting: You built an MCP server for a legacy SaaS client in one afternoon—transforming them from "we don't have an API" to "we have an AI-powered data interface." Most consultants would recommend a full rebuild. Your approach reveals how you think about modernization: build connection layers, don't rewrite everything.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You built an MCP server for a legacy SaaS client connecting Claude to their Snowflake data—transformed them from "we don't have an API" to having an AI-powered interface. Walk me through why you chose that approach. What were they struggling with before, and what made MCP the right solution?
  • You said "If your SaaS doesn't have a strategy for AI integration and MCP, you're not just behind—you're becoming irrelevant." That's a strong statement. What are you seeing that makes you so certain about this? What happens to companies that wait?
  • When you're working with a legacy company that doesn't have modern APIs, how do you help them think about where to start? What's the first thing you build to prove the concept is worth pursuing?
Topic 4: Treating GTM as a Development Project—Git Workflows for Revenue Operations

Why this is interesting: You evolved your GTM motion from "full-send" to "full-dev PLUS full-send"—treating client GTM operations like software projects with GitHub repos, branches, and PRs. Most operators still use Google Drive and spreadsheets. Your approach reveals a completely different mental model for how GTM work should be structured.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You said "any GTM motion is truly no different than any development project—you're taking data, running code through it, and molding it in a way that can be utilized to create value." When did you realize GTM work should be treated like software development? What changed in how you work?
  • Most GTM teams still use spreadsheets and Google Drive for collaboration. When you shifted to treating GTM as a development project, what was the first thing that got better? What changes when you think of revenue operations as code instead of just ops work?
  • You mentioned downloading entire CRM datasets to show clients "how messy their data actually is." What's the most common thing companies don't realize about their own data until they see it this way? What usually surprises them?
