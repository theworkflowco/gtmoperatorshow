About the GTM Operator Show

GTM Operator Show features conversations with operators who are pushing the boundaries of how GTM teams work—exploring the thinking patterns and decision frameworks behind meaningful innovation.

The Audience

The show reaches ~10,000 highly curated GTM leaders on LinkedIn—practitioners and leaders at tech startups who are actively seeking better approaches to revenue operations, process design, and systematic execution. These are operators who care about how things work, not just what to do.

Format

  • Platform: Squadcast (browser-based, no download or account needed)
  • Duration: 50-60 minutes of conversation (edited down for the final episode)
  • Style: Video podcast, conversational—just you and the host talking through the topics
  • Timing flexibility: If you have a product launch or time-sensitive announcement, we can coordinate your episode timing to align with your promotional window

You're thinking about how to make AI "create like you do" by amplifying taste and scaling expertise—not just automating tasks.

Your work on context engineering, agentic systems, and making AI preserve institutional knowledge maps perfectly to our audience of GTM leaders building intelligent systems. Your technical depth combined with GTM focus makes complex concepts (context engineering, MCP, Claude Skills) accessible and actionable.

Discussion Framework

We've identified several areas where your experience could provide unique value to GTM operators:

Topic 1: Context Management as the Real Unlock (Not Model Performance)

Why this is interesting: Most GTM leaders think AI effectiveness is about picking the right model or writing better prompts. Your insight that context management (how you structure, persist, and retrieve context) is what actually makes AI useful challenges this assumption. This maps directly to building intelligent CRM systems and institutional knowledge capture.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You've written about how Claude Code's superiority isn't about the model—it's about context management: task decomposition, persistence, and reusability. You also talk about AI that "creates like you do" and systems that compound results over time. What ties these ideas together in your mind? What did you have to learn the hard way to understand this?
  • Walk me through how you think about context management when building for a revenue team. How do you move from messy CRM data and scattered knowledge to a system that actually amplifies someone's expertise?
  • Tell me about a time when you saw a GTM team's AI system go from "this isn't much better than ChatGPT" to "this thing actually remembers how we work." What was the turning point?
Topic 2: Building Agentic Search That Actually Finds What You Need

Why this is interesting: GTM teams are drowning in data (CRM records, call transcripts, docs) but struggle to surface insights when needed. Your three-layer framework for agentic search (data/context quality → interface design → agent instructions) is a practical blueprint for making AI search actually useful. This is the "find what I forgot" problem every GTM leader has.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You shared a three-layer framework for agentic search: underlying context quality, interface design, and agent instructions. Walk me through how you developed this framework. What were you trying to solve that existing search wasn't addressing?
  • Most teams have messy CRM data, unstructured call notes, and scattered docs. When you're working with a GTM team that has this problem, where do you actually start? What's the first layer you focus on and why?
  • You mentioned the difference between "well thought-out structure" versus "throwing a big list of tools into context." Tell me about a time when you saw someone make this mistake. What did the "well thought-out" version look like instead?
Topic 3: From "AI Replaces People" to "AI Amplifies Taste"

Why this is interesting: This is a contrarian take that resonates with GTM operators building intelligent systems. Most people fear AI will commoditize expertise; you believe it will create demand for craftsmanship. This philosophical shift ("use AI to amplify your unique taste and expertise" vs. "automate and replace") changes how you design GTM systems entirely.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • Tell me about what craftsmanship means to you in the context of GTM, and why you think we'll need more of it in this age of AI. Most people think AI commoditizes expertise—what makes you see it differently?
  • You talk about "taste is the last moat" and "context is king." Walk me through how these two ideas connect. When you're building systems for a GTM team, how do you think about amplifying their taste versus just automating their process?
  • Give me a concrete example from your work where you helped someone use AI to amplify their unique expertise rather than just automate tasks. What did that look like? What changed for them?
Topic 4: Practical Prompt Engineering Patterns That Actually Work

Why this is interesting: Most prompt engineering advice is generic. You share specific, tactical patterns you've learned building systems (borrowing expertise via prompting, using AI to interview you for clarity, task decomposition). These are immediately actionable for GTM operators building workflows.

Questions we're excited to explore:

  • You shared three "high signal prompt tips" in one of your posts: borrowing expertise by asking AI who the best person to solve this problem would be, using AI to help you get clarity on the problem itself, and breaking large problems into smaller pieces. What ties these together in your mind? What did you have to learn the hard way to figure these out?
  • Walk me through your process when you're stuck on a GTM problem. How do you move from "I don't know what I'm doing" to "I have a plan"? What role does AI play in that journey?
  • You mentioned that the bottleneck often isn't the AI—it's your own lack of clarity about the problem. Tell me about a time when you realized the problem wasn't the tool, it was how you were thinking about the problem. What helped you get clear?
